Hawaiʻi is unlike anywhere else on Earth as a destination for healing. The combination of ancient indigenous traditions, a deeply rooted culture of mālama (care and stewardship), the healing power of the land itself, and a thriving community of practitioners from every tradition makes the islands a genuinely rare place to go deep into your health and wellbeing.

This guide covers every wellness modality currently practiced across Oʻahu, Maui, the Big Island, and Kauaʻi — from the deeply ancient to the cutting-edge. Whether you're a visitor seeking a meaningful healing experience, a resident looking to expand your practice, or a newcomer to the world of holistic health wondering where to begin, this is your starting point.

We've organized the 44+ modalities available in Hawaiʻi into eight broad categories, with honest descriptions of what each practice is, what it's good for, and what to expect from a session. At the end of each modality, you'll find a direct link to browse practitioners on the Hawaii Wellness directory.

What is a Wellness Modality?

A wellness modality is any structured, practiced approach to supporting human health — physical, mental, emotional, or spiritual. The term is deliberately broad: it encompasses both state-licensed clinical practices (like physical therapy or acupuncture) and traditional or ceremonial healing arts (like lomilomi or ho'oponopono) that operate outside the biomedical framework but carry profound legitimacy within their own traditions.

In Hawaiʻi specifically, the wellness landscape is unusually rich because the islands sit at a cultural crossroads. Native Hawaiian healing traditions — among the oldest continuous healing lineages in the Pacific — exist alongside the practices brought by Japanese, Chinese, Filipino, and other immigrant communities, overlaid with contemporary integrative medicine from the mainland and the deep wellness subculture that has grown around the islands' surf, yoga, and retreat communities.

What this means for you as a client or patient: there is almost certainly a modality (or combination of modalities) that maps precisely onto what you're seeking — whether that's relief from chronic pain, support through a life transition, trauma processing, spiritual deepening, or simply the experience of being held and restored.

Bodywork & Manual Therapies

8 modalities

Bodywork modalities work directly with the physical body — through touch, pressure, movement, and manipulation — to release tension, restore structural alignment, support the nervous system, and promote circulation and healing. Hawaiʻi has an exceptionally rich bodywork scene, anchored by the indigenous lomilomi tradition and enriched by world-class practitioners in massage, craniosacral, and structural therapies.

💆

Massage Therapy

Swedish · Deep Tissue · Sports · Myofascial · Rolfing · Lymphatic · Thai · Prenatal · Shiatsu · Reflexology

Massage therapy is one of the broadest categories in holistic health — "massage" is really a family of dozens of distinct techniques, each with a different mechanism and therapeutic target. Here's what you'll find in Hawaiʻi:

Swedish / Relaxation — The most widely practiced form: long, gliding strokes (effleurage), kneading, and gentle percussion to promote circulation and deep relaxation. The foundation most massage therapists learn first.

Deep Tissue — Slower, more focused pressure targeting the deeper layers of muscle and connective tissue. Excellent for chronic tension, postural patterns, and recovering from repetitive strain. Not inherently painful — a good deep tissue therapist works with your body's resistance, not against it.

Myofascial Release (MFR) — Works specifically with the fascia — the continuous web of connective tissue that wraps every structure in the body. MFR uses sustained, gentle pressure held for minutes at a time to soften and release fascial restrictions that contribute to pain, limited range of motion, and postural imbalance. Particularly effective for fibromyalgia, chronic pain, and post-surgical scarring.

Rolfing (Structural Integration) — Developed by Dr. Ida Rolf, Rolfing reorganizes the body's structure through deep manipulation of the fascial system across a systematic 10-session series. The goal is whole-body alignment within gravity — many clients report lasting postural changes, relief from chronic pain, and a new sense of ease in their body. Rolfing practitioners require specialized multi-year training.

Manual Lymphatic Drainage (MLD) — An extremely gentle, rhythmic technique that stimulates the lymphatic system to reduce swelling, support immune function, and accelerate recovery from surgery or injury. Standard protocol for post-mastectomy lymphedema; also used for general detoxification, immune support, and reducing puffiness. The pressure used is lighter than you'd expect — about the weight of a coin on skin.

Sports Massage — Applied pre- and post-event and during training cycles to support athletic performance, accelerate recovery, and prevent injury. Combines compression, cross-fiber friction, and stretching. Given Hawaiʻi's surf, triathlon, and paddling culture, many sports massage therapists have specialized knowledge of ocean and endurance sport demands.

Thai Massage — Performed fully clothed on a floor mat, Thai massage combines rhythmic compression, joint mobilization, and assisted yoga-like stretching along the body's energy lines (sen). Deeply invigorating and balancing — often described as "passive yoga." A significant Thai massage community has established itself across Maui and Oʻahu.

Prenatal Massage — Specialized massage adapted for pregnancy, using positioning bolsters and modified techniques to safely address the unique physical demands of carrying a baby. Reduces back pain, hip tension, edema, and stress. Most practitioners recommend waiting until the second trimester.

Shiatsu — Japanese bodywork that applies rhythmic pressure along the body's meridian pathways using thumbs, palms, and elbows. Performed clothed on a floor mat. Deeply balancing for the nervous system, shiatsu draws on the same energy map as acupuncture without needles.

Reflexology — Based on the principle that specific points on the feet (and hands and ears) correspond to organs, glands, and body systems, reflexology applies targeted pressure to these reflex points to promote balance and support organ function. A deeply relaxing session that clients often describe as reaching the whole body through the feet.

Lomilomi

Also: Hawaiian Massage, Loving Hands Massage

Lomilomi is Hawaiʻi's indigenous massage tradition, often translated as "loving hands." Passed down through generations of Hawaiian healing families (ʻohana), it uses long, continuous forearm strokes that flow along the body's energy pathways, combined with prayer (pule), breath, and clear healing intention. Unlike most Western massage, lomilomi is as much a spiritual practice as a physical one — the practitioner's state of presence and love is considered essential to the work. Sessions often include music, oli (chant), or prayer.

Watsu

Also: Water Therapy, Aquatic Bodywork

Watsu (water shiatsu) is a form of aquatic bodywork performed in warm water — typically a chest-deep pool heated to body temperature. The practitioner holds, moves, floats, and gently stretches the client through water, creating a state of profound release and weightlessness. The experience can be deeply meditative and often described as womb-like. In Hawaiʻi, where the ocean is a constant healing presence, Watsu practitioners create an extension of that relationship in a controlled, deeply safe environment.

Craniosacral Therapy

Also: CST, Biodynamic Craniosacral

Craniosacral therapy uses extremely light touch — often no more than the weight of a nickel — to assess and release restrictions in the craniosacral system (the membranes and cerebrospinal fluid surrounding the brain and spinal cord). Practitioners tune in to the subtle rhythmic motion of this system and work to restore free movement. It is widely used for migraines, TMJ, chronic neck/back pain, anxiety, PTSD, and post-concussion syndrome. The biodynamic form deepens into the embryological forces that shape the body.

Chiropractic

Also: Network Chiropractic, Spinal Adjustments

Chiropractic care focuses on the diagnosis and manual treatment of musculoskeletal disorders, particularly those involving the spine. Chiropractors use controlled force (adjustments) to restore proper movement to joints, relieve nerve pressure, and support the body's own healing mechanisms. In Hawaiʻi you'll find both traditional structural chiropractors and practitioners of Network Spinal Analysis (NSA) — a gentler, more somatic approach that uses light touches at specific spinal gateways to help the nervous system reorganize itself. NSA has a particularly strong following in the islands' integrative health community.

Reiki

Reiki is a Japanese energy healing technique developed in the early 20th century by Mikao Usui. Practitioners channel universal life energy (ki) through their hands — placed lightly on or just above the body — to promote relaxation, stress reduction, and the activation of the body's own healing response. Sessions are deeply relaxing and often described as deeply peaceful. Many clients report emotional releases, clarity, or a sense of reconnection.

Mind, Nervous System & Psyche

6 modalities

This category covers modalities that work primarily with the mind, psychology, and nervous system — from licensed psychotherapy to somatic approaches that bridge body and mind. Hawaiʻi has a strong mental health and integrative psychology community, with many practitioners offering trauma-informed care and nervous system work alongside (or instead of) traditional talk therapy.

💬

Psychotherapy & Counseling

CBT · EMDR · IFS · ACT · DBT · Gestalt · Psychodynamic · AEDP · Narrative · Couples · Group

Psychotherapy is one of the most varied fields in healthcare — the word covers dozens of distinct evidence-based approaches, each with a different theory of change, different techniques, and different indications. Licensed therapists in Hawaiʻi (LCSWs, LMFTs, psychologists, licensed counselors) are trained in one or more of these modalities. Here are the most common approaches you'll encounter:

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) — The most extensively researched form of psychotherapy. Works by identifying and restructuring unhelpful thought patterns (cognitions) and behaviors. Highly effective for anxiety disorders, depression, OCD, and phobias. Often time-limited (10–20 sessions) and structured.

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) — Uses bilateral stimulation (eye movements, tapping, or tones) while a client briefly recalls traumatic memories, allowing the brain to reprocess them so they lose their emotional charge. One of the most effective trauma treatments known, with strong research support for PTSD. Sessions can produce dramatic shifts in a relatively short timeframe.

Internal Family Systems (IFS) — Understands the psyche as a system of distinct "parts" — inner critics, protectors, exiles — each with its own history and motivations. The therapeutic goal is to help the client access their core Self and develop compassionate relationships with all their parts. Particularly powerful for trauma, shame, and complex developmental wounds. Widely practiced in Hawaiʻi's integrative therapy community.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) — A mindfulness-based behavioral therapy that helps clients accept difficult thoughts and feelings rather than fighting them, while committing to actions aligned with their values. Effective for depression, anxiety, chronic pain, and life transitions. Strong philosophical overlap with Buddhist psychology makes it well-suited to Hawaiʻi's contemplative communities.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) — Originally developed for borderline personality disorder, DBT is now widely used for emotional dysregulation, self-harm, eating disorders, and substance use. Combines CBT with mindfulness and a structured skills curriculum: distress tolerance, emotional regulation, interpersonal effectiveness, and radical acceptance.

Psychodynamic Therapy — Explores unconscious processes, attachment patterns, and early relational experiences as the roots of present difficulties. Less structured than CBT and typically longer-term. Particularly valuable for clients with recurring relationship patterns, identity questions, and complex developmental history.

Gestalt Therapy — Focuses on present-moment awareness, the therapeutic relationship, and the integration of unfinished emotional business. Uses active techniques — empty chair work, role play, body awareness experiments — to bring unconscious material into lived experience in the session. Gestalt has deep roots in Hawaiʻi's humanistic psychology community.

AEDP (Accelerated Experiential Dynamic Psychotherapy) — An emotion-focused, attachment-based therapy that works with transformational processes — helping clients move through and beyond trauma toward genuine flourishing. Unusually warm and relationally engaged. Particularly suited to clients who have felt misunderstood or pathologized in prior therapy.

Narrative Therapy — Helps clients externalize their problems and re-author their life stories, separating their identity from the "problem-saturated" narratives that limit them. Culturally sensitive and collaborative — well suited to working with Indigenous and Pacific Islander clients navigating intergenerational and colonization-related trauma.

Couples & Relationship Therapy — Therapists trained in Gottman Method, Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT), Imago, or other couples frameworks work with partners to improve communication, rebuild trust, navigate conflict, and deepen intimacy. Hawaiʻi's diverse multicultural partnerships and the stressors of island living make couples work particularly relevant here.

Group Therapy — Facilitated therapeutic groups offer the unique healing ingredient of witnessed belonging — being truly seen and accepted within a community. Groups exist for grief, trauma, addiction recovery, life transitions, and more. Some of the most profound therapeutic work happens in well-facilitated group containers.

Somatic Therapy

Also: Somatic Experiencing, SE, Body Psychotherapy

Somatic therapy works with the body's physical sensations, postures, and movements as the primary language for healing trauma and stress. Rather than focusing on narrative and cognitive reframing alone, somatic approaches help clients develop awareness of bodily experience — the tightening chest, the held breath, the collapsed shoulders — and work with these as direct access points to the nervous system and implicit memory. Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing (SE) is among the most widely practiced forms.

Trauma-Informed Care

Also: Trauma-Sensitive Practice

Trauma-informed care is not a single modality but a framework — a lens through which practitioners of any discipline understand and work with the effects of trauma. A trauma-informed practitioner creates safety, builds trust, empowers client agency, and adapts their approach to avoid re-traumatization. Many practitioners in Hawaiʻi hold trauma-informed certifications as a complement to their primary modality, particularly given the prevalence of historical and intergenerational trauma within Native Hawaiian and Pacific Islander communities.

Nervous System Regulation

Eastern & Integrative Medicine

6 modalities

Hawaiʻi's multicultural heritage — with deep roots in Japanese, Chinese, Filipino, and other Asian cultures — means Eastern healing traditions have been practiced here for well over a century. Acupuncture, traditional Chinese medicine, Ayurveda, naturopathic medicine, and herbalism are all widely available across the islands, often practiced by highly trained, multi-generational healers.

Acupuncture

Also: TCM Acupuncture, Dry Needling

Acupuncture involves the insertion of fine needles at specific points along the body's meridian system to regulate the flow of qi (vital energy). One of the most extensively researched alternative therapies, acupuncture has strong evidence for pain management, nausea, headaches, insomnia, and anxiety. Licensed acupuncturists in Hawaiʻi typically also practice TCM diagnosis and may use moxibustion, cupping, and herbal medicine alongside needling.

Ayurveda

Also: Panchakarma, Ayurvedic Consultation

Ayurveda is the ancient Indian system of medicine — over 5,000 years old — that understands health through the lens of three constitutional types (doshas: Vata, Pitta, Kapha) and seeks to bring the individual into balance through personalized diet, herbs, daily routine, yoga, bodywork (including abhyanga and shirodhara), and cleansing protocols (panchakarma). Ayurvedic practitioners in Hawaiʻi often integrate the island's local herbs and foods into traditional frameworks.

Common conditions addressed include autoimmune disease, thyroid disorders, digestive issues, hormonal imbalances, and fatigue. Many functional medicine doctors in Hawaiʻi hold MD or DO licenses alongside additional integrative training.

Hawaiian & Nature Traditions

5 modalities

This category honors the healing traditions rooted in the land and culture of Hawaiʻi itself. Hawaiian healing is not one thing — it is a living, evolving body of knowledge held by families, lineages, and individual practitioners who have devoted their lives to these teachings. Approaching these practices with respect, humility, and an openness to a worldview that may differ significantly from Western health frameworks is essential.

A note on cultural respect: Many Hawaiian healing practices are sacred. Some practitioners work only within Hawaiian cultural contexts or with specific communities. Others welcome all seekers. When working with Hawaiian healers, follow their lead, honor their protocols, and understand that healing within a Hawaiian framework may involve spiritual and relational dimensions that extend well beyond a single session.

Hawaiian Healing

Also: Laʻau Lapaʻau, Hoʻoponopono, Traditional Hawaiian Medicine

Hawaiian healing (laʻau lapaʻau) encompasses the full range of indigenous medical knowledge — plant medicine, massage, prayer, energy work, and ceremonial healing. Central to Hawaiian healing is the concept of pono (righteousness, balance, doing what is right) — illness is understood not only as physical malfunction but as a disruption of right relationship with self, family, community, and the natural world. Healers (kahuna lapaʻau) work across all these dimensions simultaneously.

Nature Therapy

Nature therapy — or ecotherapy — is the practice of using immersion in natural environments as a therapeutic modality. Inspired partly by the Japanese practice of shinrin-yoku (forest bathing), which has an extensive research base, nature therapy practitioners guide clients into intentional, sensory engagement with forests, coastlines, mountains, or any natural environment. Hawaiʻi's extraordinary biodiversity — from Kauaʻi's Na Pali coast to the Big Island's lava fields to Maui's bamboo forests — makes it among the finest places on Earth for this work.

Family Constellation

Also: Systemic Constellations, Hellinger Work

Family Constellation work (developed by Bert Hellinger) is a therapeutic approach that illuminates how unresolved trauma, loyalty patterns, and entanglements from previous generations influence present-day behavior and wellbeing. Sessions — held individually or in group workshops — use spatial representation (with participants or objects representing family members) to reveal hidden dynamics and find resolutions. It operates on the premise that we are shaped not only by our personal history but by the systemic field of our ancestral lineage.

Birth Doula

Also: Labor Support, Birth Companion

A birth doula is a trained non-medical companion who provides continuous physical, emotional, and informational support before, during, and after childbirth. Research consistently shows that doula support reduces cesarean rates, shortens labors, and improves birth satisfaction. In Hawaiʻi, many doulas are deeply integrated into the aloha spirit of birthing communities, supporting families across cultural backgrounds and birth settings — hospital, birth center, and home.

Midwifery

Also: Certified Nurse-Midwife, CPM, Home Birth

Midwives provide primary care for low-risk pregnancies and births, offering a model of care centered on the normalcy of birth rather than its pathology. Certified Nurse-Midwives (CNMs) are licensed to practice in hospitals and birth centers; Certified Professional Midwives (CPMs) typically attend home births. Hawaiʻi has a committed midwifery community, and the tradition of birth as a community and family event resonates deeply with Hawaiian cultural values around ʻohana (family).

How to Choose a Wellness Practitioner in Hawaiʻi

With so many modalities and practitioners available, the choice can feel overwhelming. Here are six principles that will serve you well regardless of what you're looking for.

1

Start with your intention

Are you seeking relief from a specific symptom, a deeper experience of yourself, or transformation? Your intention shapes which category of modality is most relevant.

2

Match depth to readiness

Intensive trauma work, plant medicine ceremonies, and deep transformational retreats require readiness and support structures. Not every first visit needs to go deep.

3

Check credentials — and lineage

For clinical modalities (acupuncture, physical therapy, psychotherapy), verify licensing. For traditional practices, ask about lineage and training. Both matter enormously.

4

Read reviews thoughtfully

Look for reviews that speak to how a practitioner made someone feel held and supported, not just whether the outcome was pleasant. The therapeutic relationship is the vehicle.

5

Trust the consultation

A good practitioner will offer an initial conversation or consultation. How they show up in that interaction — curious, warm, clear about boundaries — tells you a great deal.

6

Stack modalities wisely

Many people find combining modalities — e.g., acupuncture + somatic therapy, or massage + breathwork — produces results neither achieves alone. Talk to your practitioners about what might complement their work.

Lomilomi is Hawaiʻi's indigenous massage tradition, often called "loving hands" massage. It uses long, continuous forearm strokes that flow along the body's energy pathways, combined with prayer (pule), breath, and clear healing intention. Unlike most Western massage, lomilomi is equally a spiritual and physical practice — the practitioner's state of presence and love is central to the work. Sessions may include chant, music, or prayer.

Traditional hoʻoponopono is facilitated by a family elder or kahuna and involves all relevant parties together.
